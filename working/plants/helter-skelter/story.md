# Helter Skelter

Helter Skelter was never the dramatic one.

When I brought this Dendrobium home in Naples, it looked healthy from the start. The leaves were firm, the canes were upright, and there was no obvious sign of collapse or dehydration. Compared with some of the other orchids in the rescue collection, it did not seem to need saving so much as a better chance to establish itself.

The main concern was the original nursery setup. After removing the packaging and inspecting the roots, I realized this plant would need care that was different from the Phalaenopsis orchids I was more familiar with: brighter light, more drying time between waterings, good airflow, and an open growing medium.

Helter Skelter responded by doing almost nothing dramatic.

It settled in.

The leaves stayed healthy. The canes remained firm. New growth appeared quietly and kept developing.

Then the entire collection moved from Naples, Florida, to Fort Worth, Texas.

Helter Skelter traded natural Florida light for indoor grow lights, adjusted to lower humidity, survived the long move, and continued growing as though none of it had been especially difficult.

By early summer, a new basal growth had become obvious. A week later it was taller. Another week later it was opening a new leaf and thickening into a sturdy young cane.

That steady progress became Helter Skelter's story.

It has never demanded much attention. It has simply kept building: one cane, then another, then another new growth.

There are no flowers yet, but that does not feel like a failure. Every healthy cane is part of the framework that may support future blooms.

Helter Skelter has become one of the strongest growers in the collection, not because it changed overnight, but because it never stopped moving forward.
