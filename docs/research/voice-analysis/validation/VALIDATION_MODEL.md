# Validation Model

## Purpose

This document defines how hypotheses are evaluated within the Voice Analysis methodology.

Validation is the process of determining whether a hypothesis accurately represents the author's writing across the corpus.

The objective of validation is **not to prove a hypothesis correct**.

The objective is to determine whether the hypothesis survives deliberate attempts to prove it wrong.

---

# The Research Hierarchy

```text
Raw Text
    ↓
Observation
    ↓
Evidence
    ↓
Hypothesis
    ↓
Validation
    ↓
Validated Hypothesis
    ↓
Voice Model
    ↓
Derived Outputs
```

---

# Definition

Validation is the systematic evaluation of a hypothesis against the available corpus.

A validated hypothesis has survived repeated testing across multiple contexts and remains the best current explanation for the observed evidence.

Validation is an ongoing process.

No hypothesis should be considered permanently proven.

---

# Guiding Principle

Researchers should actively seek evidence that challenges a hypothesis.

Validation asks:

- Does the hypothesis hold across different writing?
- Does it hold across different time periods?
- Does it hold across different audiences?
- Does it hold across different subjects?
- Are there meaningful exceptions?
- Is there a better explanation?

The goal is confidence through rigorous testing, not confirmation.

---

# Validation Process

Each hypothesis should undergo a structured evaluation.

## 1. Review Supporting Evidence

Review all evidence currently associated with the hypothesis.

Determine whether the evidence:

- Represents multiple sources
- Covers multiple contexts
- Is sufficiently diverse
- Remains traceable to the original corpus

---

## 2. Search for Counterexamples

Researchers should intentionally search for writing that appears to contradict the hypothesis.

Possible sources include:

- Different years
- Different audiences
- Different emotional states
- Different writing formats
- Different subject matter

Counterexamples are valuable because they reveal the boundaries of a hypothesis.

---

## 3. Evaluate Exceptions

Not every exception invalidates a hypothesis.

Researchers should determine whether an exception is:

- Expected
- Context-dependent
- Rare
- Significant
- Evidence of a better explanation

Documenting exceptions is an essential part of validation.

---

## 4. Revise if Necessary

If new evidence weakens a hypothesis, researchers should:

- Refine the hypothesis
- Narrow its scope
- Split it into multiple hypotheses
- Replace it with a better explanation
- Reject it entirely

Revision reflects progress, not failure.

---

# Validation Outcomes

A validation review may result in one of the following outcomes:

## Validated

The hypothesis continues to explain the available evidence.

---

## Provisionally Supported

The evidence is encouraging but additional research is required.

---

## Revised

The hypothesis has been modified to better fit the evidence.

---

## Split

The original hypothesis has been divided into multiple, more specific hypotheses.

---

## Rejected

The available evidence no longer supports the hypothesis.

Rejected hypotheses should remain documented as part of the research history.

---

# Validation Criteria

Researchers should consider:

- Diversity of supporting evidence
- Quality of supporting evidence
- Amount of contradictory evidence
- Consistency across the corpus
- Repeatability of the observed pattern
- Alternative explanations

Validation is qualitative rather than purely numerical.

The methodology intentionally avoids rigid scoring thresholds in favor of reasoned evaluation.

---

# Confidence

Confidence reflects the strength of the current evidence.

Suggested levels:

- Very Low
- Low
- Medium
- High
- Very High

Confidence should increase only after repeated attempts to invalidate the hypothesis.

Confidence is not certainty.

---

# Revision History

Every validation should preserve its history.

Researchers should record:

- Date
- Reviewer
- Outcome
- Supporting rationale
- Changes made
- New evidence considered

The evolution of a hypothesis is valuable research data.

---

# Relationship to AI

AI may assist by:

- Searching for supporting evidence
- Identifying counterexamples
- Suggesting alternative explanations
- Comparing competing hypotheses

AI does not determine whether a hypothesis is validated.

Validation remains a human research decision.

---

# Success Criteria

The Validation Model is successful when:

- Researchers actively search for contradictory evidence.
- Validation decisions are traceable to documented evidence.
- Hypotheses evolve as the corpus expands.
- Rejected hypotheses remain documented.
- Independent researchers can understand and reproduce validation decisions.

Validation exists to strengthen the Voice Model through rigorous evaluation rather than confirmation.
