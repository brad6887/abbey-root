# Experiment Model

## Purpose

This document defines the standard structure for research experiments conducted within the Voice Analysis methodology.

Experiments validate both the research process and the conclusions produced by that process.

An experiment is a controlled application of the methodology to a defined research question.

Every experiment should follow a consistent structure to ensure repeatability, traceability, and continuous improvement of the framework.

---

# Relationship to the Research Methodology

Experiments apply the complete Voice Analysis methodology.

```text
Corpus
    ↓
Observation
    ↓
Evidence
    ↓
Hypothesis
    ↓
Validation
    ↓
Voice Model
```

Experiments are how the methodology is tested, refined, and improved.

---

# Guiding Principles

Every experiment should be:

- Reproducible
- Well documented
- Limited in scope
- Evidence-driven
- Traceable
- Open to unexpected results

The purpose of an experiment is to learn.

Unexpected findings are often more valuable than expected ones.

---

# Experiment Structure

Each experiment should contain the following sections.

---

## Experiment ID

Every experiment should have a unique identifier.

Example:

```text
EXP-001
```

---

## Title

A short descriptive name.

Example:

```text
Initial Facebook Voice Analysis
```

---

## Objective

Describe the purpose of the experiment.

Examples:

- Validate the observation process.
- Evaluate the evidence model.
- Build an initial Voice Model.
- Compare writing across time periods.

---

## Research Question

Clearly state the question being investigated.

Examples:

- Can recurring narrative patterns be identified?
- Does the observation model produce consistent results?
- Are writing characteristics stable across different years?

A good experiment should answer one primary question.

---

## Scope

Define the limits of the experiment.

Examples:

- Number of documents
- Years included
- Writing sources
- Time available
- Researchers involved

Keeping experiments intentionally small encourages rapid learning.

---

## Corpus

Document exactly what source material was analyzed.

Include information such as:

- Source
- Date range
- Number of documents
- Selection method
- Known limitations

---

## Methodology

Describe how the methodology was applied.

Possible topics include:

- Observation process
- Evidence selection
- Hypothesis development
- Validation approach

If deviations from the standard methodology occur, document them.

---

## Observations

Summarize the observations produced during the experiment.

This section should focus on descriptive findings rather than conclusions.

---

## Evidence

Summarize the evidence selected from the observations.

Include:

- Significant recurring patterns
- Important exceptions
- Unexpected findings

---

## Hypotheses

Document all hypotheses generated during the experiment.

For each hypothesis include:

- Description
- Status
- Supporting evidence
- Contradictory evidence
- Confidence

---

## Validation

Describe how hypotheses were evaluated.

Include:

- Counterexamples examined
- Exceptions discovered
- Revisions made
- Validation outcome

---

## Results

Summarize the primary findings.

This section should answer the original research question.

---

## Lessons Learned

Describe what the experiment taught about:

- The corpus
- The methodology
- The research process
- The author being studied

Unexpected lessons should be documented even if they fall outside the original objective.

---

## Framework Improvements

Document recommended improvements to the methodology.

Possible improvements include:

- New observation categories
- Better evidence organization
- Improved validation techniques
- Documentation updates
- New research questions

Experiments improve the framework as much as they improve the research.

---

## Future Work

Document logical follow-up experiments.

Examples:

- Expand the corpus
- Compare multiple authors
- Analyze a different writing style
- Validate findings with additional researchers

---

# Experiment Lifecycle

Suggested lifecycle:

```text
Planned
    ↓
In Progress
    ↓
Analysis Complete
    ↓
Reviewed
    ↓
Framework Updated
    ↓
Closed
```

Framework updates should occur before closing an experiment whenever practical.

---

# Relationship to AI

Artificial intelligence may assist throughout an experiment by:

- Identifying candidate observations
- Organizing evidence
- Suggesting hypotheses
- Finding counterexamples
- Summarizing findings

AI serves as a research assistant.

Researchers remain responsible for experimental design, interpretation, and validation.

---

# Success Criteria

A successful experiment should:

- Answer a clearly defined research question.
- Produce traceable observations and evidence.
- Improve understanding of the author.
- Improve the Voice Analysis methodology.
- Be reproducible by another researcher.

Every completed experiment should leave both the research and the framework stronger than before.
