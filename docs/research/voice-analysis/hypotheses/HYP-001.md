# Hypothesis

## Description

Within Facebook posts that contain a discernible impossible, absurd, fictional, or strongly exaggerated humorous premise, the author frequently uses literal, procedural, or ordinary language, allowing contrast between the situation and presentation style to create the humorous effect.

## Status

Proposed

## Supporting Evidence

- EVID-001

The evidence demonstrates repeated examples where impossible situations are described using ordinary narrative language.

A deterministic 140-post prevalence sample found:

- 16 posts with a qualifying absurd-humorous premise.
- 11 with deadpan delivery.
- A conditional rate of 68.75%.
- A 95% Wilson interval of 44.40% to 85.84%.

## Contradictory Evidence

Five sampled absurd-premise posts used overt or explanatory delivery rather than deadpan delivery.

## Confidence

Medium

Confidence is medium within the sampled Facebook scope. The conditional-frequency threshold was met, but only sixteen sampled posts contained a qualifying absurd-humorous premise.

## Open Questions

- Does this pattern continue across the full Facebook corpus?
- Does the pattern appear in more recent writing?
- Does the same technique appear in technical writing?
- Are there examples where absurd situations are presented with a different style?
- Is the humor dependent on the reader recognizing the contrast?

## Validation Requirements

Future validation should examine:

- Different time periods
- Different writing formats
- Different audiences
- Counterexamples where absurd subjects are presented differently
