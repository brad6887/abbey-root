# Hypothesis Model

## Purpose

This document defines how hypotheses are developed, evaluated, and maintained within the Voice Analysis methodology.

Hypotheses explain recurring patterns observed in an author's writing.

Unlike observations and evidence, hypotheses are interpretive. They attempt to explain what the collected evidence suggests while remaining open to revision or rejection.

---

# The Research Hierarchy

```text
Raw Text
    ↓
Observation
    ↓
Evidence
    ↓
Hypothesis
    ↓
Validation
    ↓
Validated Hypothesis
    ↓
Voice Model
    ↓
Derived Outputs
```

---

# Definition

A hypothesis is a proposed explanation for one or more recurring patterns observed in the corpus.

A hypothesis should be:

- Testable
- Falsifiable
- Supported by evidence
- Open to revision

A hypothesis is never considered true simply because supporting evidence exists.

Its strength comes from surviving repeated attempts to disprove it.

---

# Characteristics of Good Hypotheses

Good hypotheses are:

- Specific
- Evidence-based
- Testable
- Narrow in scope
- Open to revision

Poor hypotheses are:

- Vague
- Based on intuition
- Difficult to evaluate
- Too broad
- Impossible to disprove

---

# Examples

Weak:

> Brad is funny.

Better:

> Brad frequently closes technical explanations with a humorous observation.

Weak:

> Brad writes conversationally.

Better:

> Brad frequently addresses the reader directly using second-person language.

---

# Hypothesis Record

Each hypothesis should include:

- Hypothesis ID
- Description
- Status
- Supporting Evidence
- Contradictory Evidence
- Confidence
- Open Questions
- Revision History

Example:

```text
Hypothesis ID:
H-003

Description:
The author usually introduces technical concepts by first describing a real situation.

Status:
Provisional

Supporting Evidence:

- E-004
- E-012
- E-018

Contradictory Evidence:

- E-021

Confidence:
Medium

Open Questions:

Does this pattern also occur in personal writing?
```

---

# Hypothesis Status

Suggested lifecycle:

- Proposed
- Under Investigation
- Provisionally Supported
- Validated
- Revised
- Rejected

A rejected hypothesis is still valuable because it documents what the evidence does not support.

---

# Confidence

Confidence should reflect the quality of the supporting evidence rather than the researcher's belief.

Possible levels include:

- Very Low
- Low
- Medium
- High
- Very High

Confidence should increase only after deliberate attempts to invalidate the hypothesis.

---

# Relationships

A hypothesis may:

- Depend on multiple pieces of evidence.
- Support higher-level hypotheses.
- Be replaced by a more accurate hypothesis.
- Split into multiple narrower hypotheses.

Relationships between hypotheses should be documented where appropriate.

---

# Revision

Hypotheses are expected to evolve.

Researchers should revise them when:

- New evidence appears.
- Counterexamples emerge.
- Better explanations are developed.
- The corpus expands.

Revision history should be preserved whenever practical.

---

# Relationship to Validation

Validation exists to challenge hypotheses.

Researchers should actively search for:

- Counterexamples
- Different writing contexts
- Different time periods
- Different audiences
- Exceptions to the proposed pattern

The objective is not to confirm the hypothesis.

The objective is to determine whether it survives careful scrutiny.

---

# Relationship to AI

AI may assist in suggesting candidate hypotheses.

Researchers remain responsible for evaluating them against documented evidence.

---

# Success Criteria

The Hypothesis Model is successful when:

- Every hypothesis is supported by traceable evidence.
- Competing hypotheses can be compared objectively.
- Researchers can explain why a hypothesis exists.
- Rejected hypotheses remain documented for future reference.
