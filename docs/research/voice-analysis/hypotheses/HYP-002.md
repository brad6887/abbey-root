# Hypothesis

## Description

The author frequently compresses ideas by relying on shared context and reader inference rather than explicitly explaining every detail.

When the intended meaning can be understood by the audience, the author often chooses minimal language over additional explanation.

## Status

Proposed

## Supporting Evidence

- EVID-002

The evidence demonstrates repeated examples where complete ideas, reactions, and narratives are communicated using minimal text.

The examples include:

- Single-word responses.
- Short contextual statements.
- Compressed sequences of events.

The evidence also includes a counterexample showing that concise expression is a preference rather than an absolute rule.

## Contradictory Evidence

- EVID-002 includes examples where the author uses longer explanations when additional context is required.

This does not necessarily contradict the hypothesis because the proposed pattern concerns compression when shared context allows it.

## Confidence

Low

The hypothesis is based on a limited sample from the first 100 chronological Facebook posts.

Additional validation is required across:

- The complete Facebook corpus.
- Different time periods.
- Different writing formats.

## Open Questions

- Does this pattern appear in technical writing?
- Does the author intentionally reduce explanation when communicating with familiar audiences?
- Does this characteristic contribute to the humorous effect observed in other hypotheses?
- Are there situations where the author deliberately avoids relying on reader inference?

## Validation Requirements

Future validation should examine:

- Different audiences.
- Different writing formats.
- Longer-form writing.
- Examples where the audience has less shared context.

The hypothesis should be tested against examples where additional explanation would normally be expected.
