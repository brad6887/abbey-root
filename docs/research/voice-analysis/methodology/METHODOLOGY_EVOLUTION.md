# Methodology Evolution

## Purpose

This document defines how the Voice Analysis methodology evolves over time.

The methodology is expected to improve through practical application rather than theoretical design alone.

Changes should be driven by evidence gathered during research experiments, not by assumptions about future needs.

The objective is to ensure the methodology becomes more accurate, more reproducible, and easier to apply while preserving the integrity of previous research.

---

# Guiding Philosophy

The methodology is never considered complete.

It is considered *current*.

Every revision should represent the best understanding available at that point in time.

Framework evolution is expected.

Framework instability is not.

---

# Guiding Principles

## Experience Before Expansion

New methodology should emerge from repeated research experience.

Avoid adding new processes simply because they seem useful.

Instead, ask:

- Did multiple experiments expose the same limitation?
- Would the proposed change improve reproducibility?
- Does the change reduce ambiguity?
- Does the change simplify future research?

---

## Evidence Over Opinion

Methodology changes should be supported by documented observations from completed experiments.

Examples include:

- Researchers consistently reaching different conclusions.
- Repeated uncertainty during validation.
- Missing documentation.
- Workflow friction.
- Poor reproducibility.

Changes should solve demonstrated problems.

---

## Simplicity

The methodology should become simpler whenever possible.

Additional documentation should only exist when it improves:

- Clarity
- Consistency
- Objectivity
- Reproducibility

Complexity should require justification.

---

## Backward Compatibility

Existing research should remain understandable after methodology changes.

When practical:

- Preserve historical terminology.
- Maintain traceability.
- Document breaking changes.
- Explain migration strategies.

Older research should remain valuable.

---

# Sources of Improvement

Methodology improvements may originate from:

- Completed experiments
- Researcher feedback
- Validation reviews
- New corpus types
- Improvements in AI-assisted research
- Comparative author studies
- Documentation reviews

Repeated findings are stronger indicators than isolated observations.

---

# Proposed Methodology Changes

Every proposed change should include:

## Problem

What problem was observed?

---

## Evidence

Which experiments or research activities demonstrated the problem?

---

## Proposed Change

Describe the recommended improvement.

---

## Expected Benefits

Examples:

- Better consistency
- Improved reproducibility
- Less ambiguity
- Reduced effort
- Better research quality

---

## Risks

Document potential drawbacks.

Examples:

- Increased complexity
- Additional researcher effort
- Breaking historical compatibility
- Reduced flexibility

Every methodology change represents a tradeoff.

---

# Types of Changes

Examples include:

- New observation categories
- Improved evidence organization
- Updated hypothesis lifecycle
- Revised validation procedures
- New experiment templates
- Additional Voice Model sections
- Documentation restructuring

Changes should remain incremental whenever possible.

---

# Versioning

Methodology revisions should be versioned.

Each revision should record:

- Version
- Date
- Summary
- Rationale
- Documents affected
- Significant workflow changes

Version history provides valuable insight into the maturity of the framework.

---

# Review Cycle

The methodology should be reviewed periodically rather than continuously.

Suggested review triggers include:

- Completion of significant experiments
- Introduction of new corpus types
- Major framework revisions
- Discovery of recurring research issues

Routine review encourages thoughtful evolution rather than constant modification.

---

# Relationship to Experiments

Experiments are the primary mechanism for improving the methodology.

Every completed experiment should answer:

- What worked well?
- What caused confusion?
- What should change?
- What should remain unchanged?

Framework improvements should be documented before an experiment is formally closed whenever practical.

---

# Relationship to AI

Artificial intelligence may assist by:

- Identifying recurring workflow issues
- Summarizing lessons across experiments
- Suggesting organizational improvements
- Comparing methodology revisions

AI may recommend changes.

Researchers determine whether those changes become part of the methodology.

---

# Success Criteria

The methodology is successful when it:

- Produces consistent research across different researchers.
- Improves through repeated experimentation.
- Remains understandable as it grows.
- Preserves historical research.
- Balances stability with continuous improvement.

The methodology should evolve deliberately, guided by evidence gathered through its own application.
